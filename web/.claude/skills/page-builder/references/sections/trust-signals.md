# Trust Signals

Trust signals reduce the specific doubt a visitor has at a specific moment. The art is **matching the proof type to the doubt, and placing it next to the claim it backs** — not dumping every logo into one band.

## Doubt → proof mapping

| Visitor doubt | Proof that answers it |
|---------------|-----------------------|
| "Is this real / will they vanish?" | Logos, customer count, years in business, real address/phone |
| "Does it actually work?" | Testimonials with names+photos, case results, before/after, metrics |
| "Is it for someone like me?" | Testimonials from a matching segment, audience-specific examples |
| "Is it any good?" | Ratings/reviews, awards, press mentions, expert endorsement |
| "Will I lose money/time?" | Guarantee, free trial, refund policy, no-card-required |
| "Is my data safe?" | Security badges, compliance marks, privacy statement |
| "Is the source legitimate?" (content/halacha) | Author credentials, cited sources (מקורות), rabbinic/expert backing |

## Proof types, ranked by persuasive weight

1. **Specific named testimonial** with a real photo and a concrete result. Strongest.
2. **Quantified outcome** ("cut review time 40%", "12,000 families use it").
3. **Recognizable logos / press.**
4. **Ratings and review counts** (with link to the source).
5. **Authority/credentials** (certifications, endorsements, author bio).
6. **Risk reversers** (guarantee, free trial).
7. **Generic badges** ("100% secure"). Weakest — use sparingly, back with substance.

Specificity beats volume. One detailed testimonial outperforms ten one-liners.

## Placement rules

- **Immediately after the hero promise:** a light trust strip (logos / one stat / rating). Confirms legitimacy before the reader invests scroll.
- **Adjacent to each major claim:** the proof for a claim sits in or beside the section making it — not parked elsewhere.
- **Before and at each CTA:** people decide at the CTA; put the most relevant proof right before it.
- **At objection points:** guarantees and FAQ-style reassurance go where hesitation peaks (near pricing, near signup).
- **Footer:** persistent, low-key signals (security, registration, contact reality).

## Quality bar

- Use **real** names, photos, numbers. Never fabricate testimonials or invent metrics — it is dishonest and legally risky. If real proof doesn't exist yet, design the slot and flag it as a content TODO rather than inventing.
- Attribute claims (link reviews to their platform; cite sources for factual/halachic statements).
- Photos of real people outperform illustrations for testimonials.

## Anti-patterns

- A wall of 20 grey logos that says nothing specific.
- Anonymous testimonials ("— a happy customer").
- Vague stats with no source ("99% satisfaction").
- Trust badges that link nowhere.
- Stuffing all proof into one section far from the claims it supports.

## i18n / RTL reminders

- Translate testimonials and proof copy; a stat in the wrong language reads as imported/fake.
- Numerals: keep digits LTR even inside RTL text; use locale-appropriate thousands separators.
- Logo strips and rating stars mirror in RTL — use logical layout (see `../i18n-rtl.md`).
